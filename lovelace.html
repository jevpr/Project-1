Ada Lovelace: the first computer programmer

Lovelace was born in the mid-1800s and contributed inportant insights to the world of computing. Her annotations on the paper Elements of Charles Babbage's Analytical Machine were forerunners to the transition from calculation to computation. For that reason, she has been called "a prophet of the computer age".

Keep scrolling to find out more!

10th December 1815: Born

Augusta Ada Gordon is born to Anne Milbanke, an educational reformer, active abolitionist, and campaigner for the womens rights movement.

1828: An interest in flight

Aged 12, Ada develops an interest in flight. She wants to make her own pair of wings, and experiments with different materials. She decides to write a book about her investigations: Flyology.

1832-33: Meets Charles Babbage

At 17, Ada’s mathematical abilities had begun to emerge. Her tutor Mary Somerville (a noted 19th-centruy researcher and scientific author) introduces her to Charles Babbage - a mathematician, philosopher, inventor, and mechanical engineer. Babbage demonstrated a small working section of his Difference Engine.

1835: Marries Lord Lovelace

At 19, Ada marries Lord King, later to become Earl of Lovelace. This is how Ada gets her well-known surname: Lovelace.

1841:Shows first-rate potential

At the time, women in the UK were not allowed to attend university. However, Ada’s tutor - the mathematician Augustus De Morgan - writes in a letter to her mother: “ Had any young beginner, about to go to Cambridge, shown the same power, I should have prophesied that his aptitude at grasping the strong points and the real difficulties of the first principles […]would have certainly made him an original mathematical investigator, perhaps of first-rate eminence.” – Ada: A Life and Legacy, p. 82

1843: Translates and annotates 'Elements of Charles Babbage's Analytical Machine'

Ada translates and annotates an article written by the Italian mathematician and engineer, Luigi Menabrea. It’s English title was: “Elements of Charles Babbage’s Analytical Machine”. Her annotations are published in notes at the end of the translation, labelled A-G. These notes are three times longer than the original manuscript.

In Note A, Ada writes that the operating mechanism ‘might act upon other things besides number… [it] might compose elaborate and scientific pieces of music of any degree of complexity or extent.’ She makes the same suggestion for graphics and language.

The idea that machines might be able to manipulate symbols and follow rules, and that numbers could represent something other than quantity, is the sign of a fundamental transition from calculation to computation.

In Note G, Ada Lovelace creates an early computer algorithm, designed to calculate a sequence of numbers using Babbage’s hypothetical analytical engine. Note G is generally agreed by many to be the first algorithm designed specifically for a computer.
